# A Modern Day Improper Inquisition Into A Man
A fictional dialogue that takes place during enhanced interrogation, probably not legal according to many. Generated by Claude Sonnet 4.6.
-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The snoring was obscene — a wet, rattling sound that filled the basement like a broken radiator. Trevor's chin rested against his chest, his wrists bound behind the chair back, dried blood crusted along his left temple. His shirt was torn at the collar. Beneath his swollen eye, the skin had gone the color of a bruised plum.

The man stood over him for a moment, watching. Then he picked up the bucket.

The water hit Trevor like a verdict.

He lurched forward against the ropes, gasping, blinking, his face a sheet of shock that almost immediately collapsed into fury. He found the man with his eyes and snarled — a raw, animal sound from somewhere low in his chest.

"Is that probable cause enough for you to be angry?" the man asked, his voice carrying the patience of someone who had rehearsed this. "If you're going to investigate me. Try me for crimes. You should untie yourself first."

Trevor shook the water from his face and laughed — not warmly. "I already know this won't end well for me," he said, his voice hoarse. "And if there is a God, your life will end just as terribly."

The man tilted his head. "What do you know about God?" A quiet contempt settled into his expression. "You were never at my church. You might as well be a heretic to me."

The word hung in the damp air between them.

Trevor looked around the room slowly, cataloguing his situation with the tired eyes of a man who had already accepted the math of it. "So," he said. "How is this going to end?"

The man pulled a chair close and sat down in it backwards, arms folded across the top rung, face level with Trevor's. He took his time before speaking.

"Tell you what," he said. "Your words will decide." He paused. "I was debating with myself heavily on what to do with you. Your men went on the offensive. Decided to play vigilante. Decided it was *just* — " he let the word curl with disdain — "to carry out cruel and unusual punishment against people like me. And they did it through means that left little trace afterwards. Facts." His eyes didn't blink. "Do you know what evil is?"

Trevor let a slow smile spread across his battered face. He chuckled — a sound like gravel shifting. "Answer that yourself," he said. "If I was responsible for your problems like that, let me just say — " a pause, deliberate, almost fond — "I'm glad I made an impression on you."

Something moved behind the man's eyes. He steadied himself before continuing.

"If your house got burned down," he said, "you'd be angry, right?" His voice had taken on a cadence now, methodical, like a man laying stones. "If you kept having to fear that people were going to burn it down again, you'd call that terrorism, right? And if you were able to stop someone from trying to burn your house down, and they turned around and played the victim — you'd say *screw em*, right?" He leaned forward slightly. "And if you people signed a legal agreement with someone, and then turned around and struck a deal with their rivals — you wouldn't call that dishonorable, right?" The sarcasm in the final word was a blade. "This is how you people are. People like you made blood enemies with people like me." His voice dropped. "Remember that."

Trevor held his gaze for a long moment. When he spoke, his tone was almost philosophical — a man not so much defending himself as describing the weather.

"You know this world isn't big enough to suit everyone," he said. "You know people in high places want to stay in high places." He raised an eyebrow, the one above his good eye. "Are you really surprised that not everyone is a friend to you?"

The man's jaw tightened. The muscle along the hinge of it flexed once, then went still. He stood from the chair slowly, straightening to his full height.

"We're going to talk some more," he said quietly. "In a few days."

Then his fist came down like a gavel, and the court was adjourned.
